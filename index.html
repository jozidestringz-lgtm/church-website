Our History

✝
URCSA

The Uniting Reformed Church in Southern Africa

The Uniting Reformed Church in Southern Africa (URCSA) has its roots in the deeply racially divided history of South African Christianity. The URCSA was formed by the union of the Black and Coloured Nederduits Gereformeerde Kerk mission churches, tracing its origins back to 1652 when the Dutch established a station at the Cape and introduced slavery. In 1857, the Dutch Reformed Church synod decided to hold separate services for Coloured members, and by 1881, the Dutch Reformed Mission Church was formally established, followed by the Dutch Reformed Church in Africa in 1951 for Black members.

Both the DRMC and the DRCA remained under the control of the Dutch Reformed Church for decades, with Black and Coloured congregations kept structurally separate across South Africa. It was not until April 14, 1994 that the DRMC and the DRCA formally merged to create the URCSA, a Reformed Christian denomination that now encompasses roughly 1.2 million members across hundreds of congregations in South Africa, Namibia, and Lesotho.

The URCSA's greatest impact lies in its theological and political witness against apartheid and its commitment to justice and reconciliation. A defining milestone was the Belhar Confession, drafted in 1982 and ratified in 1986, which declared that the gospel demands unity in the church across racial and ethnic divisions and calls the church to oppose all forms of oppression. Through its ongoing commitment to unity, reconciliation, and justice, the URCSA continues to serve as a spiritual home and advocate for marginalized communities across Southern Africa.
